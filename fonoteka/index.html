Fonoteka

A simple app that allows you to take
your favorite music albums with you.

I was involved in making of Fonoteka from day one until release. I conceptualized the idea, created prototypes and mockups, designed interactions and interface.

Fonoteka is not like "another streaming music app". Put simply, it does just one thing — give access to several offline synced albums. And for some users, this one thing is better than using an all-in-one monster music app.

Unbundling music

Fonoteka was a first mobile app from Zvooq music service. It was intended to be a part of the ecosystem of multiple apps, where each application is focused on a particular type of interaction and/or user behavior. This idea is being successfully implemented by other companies: Messenger – Paper – Facebook, Swarm – Foursquare, and others.

There are many kinds of interaction with music or around it. People connect to music in different ways.

The mission of Fonoteka is to reveal (or bring back) the importance of music album as a form of art and make user into closer contact with the music.

Understanding users

I talked to my people I know, especially those whom I consider music lovers, experts or addicts. I asked my colleagues and some random people. We had a few brainstorming sessions. And of course, I have reflected and meditated on my personal connection with music. Some insights were revealed and I wrote them down:

  • During a daily commute, there's not much time and attention for music discovery. Discovering good music takes time and mental effort. In a mobile context, it's more important to have something known to be good.
  • Some people who are seriously into music prefer albums to automated streams of recommended content or celebrity playlists.
  • For any of those album listeners, there's normally a "personal album chart" of few current favorites. This personal chart is slowly changing, but rarely all at once.
  • Glitches and interruptions during streaming via mobile network are very annoying and disruptive. Russia is a big country and network coverage is far from perfect.

Inspiration

How to put the personal top chart into the app and make that music feel close and real? With car compact disc changers ten years ago, you would have to go around the car to load CDs into the trunk. It was an awkward thing to do, but there was a good side of it: stronger connection to the music, once it's there in the changer.

This connection to music is not there anymore with most contemporary streaming services, gigabytes of pirated mp3s and automated radio stations.

Car CD changers of the 90s, commuters in Moscow metro

I wanted to bring the feeling of real and accessible music to daily commuters, people who live on relatively high speed and don't have much time and attention to select music when they have time to listen (on the way). Another good interface from the past: hardware players that could have been operated without taking the device out of the pocket. I wanted to get closer to that.

Album interactions

Limiting the number of simultaneously accessible albums gave us more freedom with subscription pricing, lowering the threshold for new users. But what if we think of a cd changer for a second, and add more weight and value to these few albums, to counterweight for the limitation?

Early sketch: use dragging to make the album feel more tactile. This idea did not survive.

In the first concept, I tried to use dragging to make album covers feel more "real" while loading album into the slot, just like you put a tape into a cassette deck. This could work for selecting and loading music, but small slots at the bottom of the screen were not usable for playback and access.

More lucky concept: horizontally swipable album cards, up and down swipes for primary and secondary album actions

In the next prototype I extended slots area outside the viewport horizontally. This way we could have a nice beautiful album cover in the middle of the screen. Left and right swipe gestures were used for selecting a slot. Up and down swipes nicely fit to "Play" and "Load" actions. It seemed a better solution and was used in the prototypes.

Interface demo

Cards, layers, fluid animations work together to bring continuity.

App concept

Key points of the finalized concept, as it was seen before production:

  • No streaming, instead “borrow” up to 5 albums per day to listen offline.
  • Limited number of slots to put albums — “CD changer on-the-go” experience.
  • Search Zvooq catalog, or select albums from your collection on Zvooq web.
  • Minimum buttons, maximum gestures for easy and fliud interaction.
  • $1 monthly subscription: ten times cheaper than full-featured services. The restrictions in the app allowed Zvooq and rightsholders to agree on the unbeatable subscription price tier for a licensed music app — $1 per month, making is stand out of other legal music products.

Evaluation and improvement

In the next releases, we improved a number of things, biggest of them are:

Music discovery for new users. The app worked nicely for existing users who had a collection of music from web service. For new users it offered only a search field to select new music. So we added a row of horizontally swipeable recommendations, to make it easier for new users to start.

Migration to one price tier with a recurring subscription. In the first release the app offered two one-time purchases: $1 for 3 slots and $2 for 7 slots. Quite soon it became clear that 3 slots just did not feel like something cool and worth paying for, and 7 slots felt much better, even a bit too wide. We switched to recurring subscription and managed to get better terms: $1 recurring payments and 5 slots.
